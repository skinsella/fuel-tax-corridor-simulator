1 The idea

How a price corridor works

▲

Oil price above corridor

Excise is automatically cut in proportion to how far Brent exceeds the upper bound. Drivers pay less at the pump.

●

Oil price inside corridor

Excise stays at the normal rate. No fiscal cost, no intervention needed. The rule is dormant.

▼

Oil price below corridor

Excise rises modestly, rebuilding fiscal capacity. Pump prices are already low, so the effect on drivers is small.

2 The comparison

Corridor vs the 2022 emergency excise cuts

What actually happened

On 9 March 2022 the government cut petrol excise by 20 c/L and diesel by 15 c/L as Brent surged past €100/bbl. The cuts were kept in place for fifteen months and restored in three steps through mid-2023.

What a corridor would have done

With the corridor set above, excise would have adjusted automatically — cutting deeper when oil was highest, easing back as prices fell, and switching off entirely once Brent returned inside the band.

4 Making it happen

How Ireland could implement a corridor in 2026

A corridor stabiliser is not a new idea internationally, but Ireland has never legislated one. Below is a practical roadmap for moving from this prototype to an operational policy, based on current Irish fiscal architecture and the legislative calendar.

Week 1

Design paper and legal confirmation

Department of Finance drafts a two-page corridor specification: formula, Brent reference source, adjustment coefficient, excise floor. Revenue Commissioners confirm that Section 96 of the Finance Act 1999 already grants the Minister power to vary mineral oil tax rates by order — no new primary legislation required.

Week 2

Fiscal scoring and FAC review

Department of Finance scores the corridor against a baseline of unchanged excise, using the simulator's own model. The Fiscal Advisory Council receives a briefing on the contingent liability and its treatment under fiscal rules. DETE/NEMO confirms the Brent data feed for operational use.

Week 3

Ministerial order and industry notice

The Minister signs a statutory instrument setting the corridor bounds and adjustment coefficient. Revenue Commissioners issue an eBrief to fuel distributors with the new rate schedule and at least 5 working days' notice before the first adjusted rate takes effect.

Week 4

Operational launch

The corridor-adjusted excise rate takes effect. Revenue Commissioners begin fortnightly recalculation based on the trailing Brent average. The Department of Finance publishes a live corridor dashboard showing the current reference price, excise adjustment, and cumulative fiscal impact.

Ongoing

Transparency and review

Fortnightly rate updates published automatically. A formal review after 12 months assesses whether the corridor parameters remain appropriate and whether the mechanism should be legislated permanently in the next Finance Bill.

Key design choices for the legislation

Reference price

A trailing 4-week average of ICE Brent in euro avoids reacting to single-day spikes. The ECB EUR/USD reference rate converts the dollar-denominated contract. The Central Bank or NEMO can publish the calculated reference weekly.

Adjustment frequency

Monthly adjustment balances responsiveness against administrative burden. Fortnightly would track shocks faster but increases compliance cost for distributors. The enabling provision should allow the Minister to move to fortnightly by order if needed during a crisis.

Excise floor

The corridor should never reduce excise below zero or below a minimum rate set in the legislation. EU Energy Taxation Directive minima (€0.359/L for petrol, €0.330/L for diesel) provide a natural floor. The floor protects the environmental price signal embedded in fuel tax.

Sunset and review

A two-year sunset clause forces the Oireachtas to actively renew the corridor. This prevents a temporary measure becoming permanent without scrutiny and gives the Fiscal Advisory Council a natural review point.

Carbon tax interaction

The corridor adjusts the mineral oil tax component only. The carbon tax component continues on its legislated trajectory to €100/tonne by 2030. This preserves the carbon price signal while stabilising the discretionary excise element.

Fiscal buffer

During periods when Brent is below the lower bound, the corridor raises excise modestly. Revenue from these periods should be ring-fenced in the National Oil Reserves Agency or a dedicated stabilisation fund to pre-fund the cost of future excise cuts.

What the Minister needs from officials

Department of Finance

Formal costing paper with central, high-oil and low-oil scenarios. Draft heads of Bill for the Finance Bill corridor provisions. Confirmation of fiscal rules treatment of the contingent liability.

Revenue Commissioners

Operational feasibility assessment for monthly rate changes. IT systems readiness for automated rate publication. Distributor notification protocol.

DETE / NEMO

Weekly publication of the EUR-denominated trailing Brent average. Data feed to Revenue for automated calculation.

Fiscal Advisory Council

Assessment of the corridor's interaction with the expenditure benchmark and the Medium-Term Objective. Guidance on whether corridor costs should be treated as cyclical or discretionary.

The legislative vehicle exists: Section 96 of the Finance Act 1999 already allows mineral oil tax rates to be varied by Ministerial order. The policy question is not whether the mechanism is legally possible, but whether the government wants a rule-based commitment rather than discretionary intervention. The 2022 experience showed that ad-hoc cuts are slower to start, politically difficult to reverse, and more expensive than a calibrated automatic rule would have been.
