2 The comparison

Corridor vs the 2022 emergency excise cuts

What actually happened

On 9 March 2022 the government cut petrol excise by 20 c/L and diesel by 15 c/L as Brent surged past €100/bbl. The cuts were restored in four steps from June 2023 to August 2024 — nearly two and a half years of reduced rates.

What a corridor would have done

With the corridor set above, excise would have adjusted automatically — cutting deeper when oil was highest, easing back as prices fell, and switching off entirely once Brent returned inside the band.

4 Making it happen

How Ireland could implement a corridor in 2026

Ireland has no existing power to vary excise rates automatically. The Minister cannot change mineral oil tax by order — each change requires a Dáil vote. But there are two tracks: an emergency Financial Resolution for immediate activation (valid four months), and a Finance Act section for a permanent formula-based mechanism.

Week 1

Design paper and AG advice

Department of Finance drafts the corridor specification: formula, Brent reference source, adjustment coefficient, excise floor. The Attorney General advises on whether a formula-based rate in a Financial Resolution satisfies the Cityview Press test for delegated legislation under Article 15.2 of the Constitution.

Week 2

Fiscal scoring and IFAC briefing

Department of Finance scores the corridor against a baseline of unchanged excise. The Fiscal Advisory Council receives a briefing on the contingent liability and its treatment under the net expenditure path. SEAI confirms the Brent data feed.

Week 3

Dáil Financial Resolution

The Dáil passes a Financial Resolution under the Provisional Collection of Taxes Act 1927, inserting corridor-adjusted rates into Schedule 2 of the Finance Act 1999. This has immediate statutory effect but lapses after four months unless confirmed in a Finance Act. Revenue issue an eBrief to distributors.

Week 4

Operational launch

The corridor-adjusted excise rate takes effect. Revenue Commissioners begin fortnightly recalculation based on the trailing Brent average published by SEAI. The Department of Finance publishes a live corridor dashboard.

Budget night

Permanent legislation

The Finance Bill inserts a new Section 96A into the Finance Act 1999, making the corridor permanent. It defines the formula, the Brent reference index, the corridor bounds, the adjustment coefficient, the EU-minimum floor, and empowers the Minister to activate adjustments by order within those bounds — following the Italian model.

Key design choices for the legislation

Reference price

A trailing 4-week average of ICE Brent in euro avoids reacting to single-day spikes. The ECB EUR/USD reference rate converts the dollar-denominated contract. SEAI or the Central Bank can publish the calculated reference weekly.

Adjustment frequency

Monthly adjustment balances responsiveness against administrative burden. Fortnightly would track shocks faster but increases compliance cost for distributors. The Finance Act should empower the Minister to move to fortnightly recalculation by order during a crisis.

Excise floor

The corridor should never reduce excise below zero or below a minimum rate set in the legislation. EU Energy Taxation Directive minima (€0.359/L for petrol, €0.330/L for diesel) provide a natural floor. The floor protects the environmental price signal embedded in fuel tax.

Sunset and review

A two-year sunset clause forces the Oireachtas to actively renew the corridor. This prevents a temporary measure becoming permanent without scrutiny and gives the Fiscal Advisory Council a natural review point.

Carbon tax interaction

The corridor adjusts the mineral oil tax component only. The carbon tax component continues on its legislated trajectory to €100/tonne by 2030. This preserves the carbon price signal while stabilising the discretionary excise element.

Fiscal buffer

During periods when Brent is below the lower bound, the corridor raises excise modestly. This revenue should be ring-fenced in a dedicated stabilisation fund to pre-fund the cost of future excise cuts — the Italian accise mobili approach of matching VAT windfalls to excise relief.

What the Minister needs from officials

Revenue Commissioners

SEAI

Weekly publication of the EUR-denominated trailing Brent average (ICE front-month converted at ECB reference rate). Data feed to Revenue for automated calculation.

Fiscal Advisory Council

Assessment of the corridor's interaction with the net expenditure path under Ireland's Medium-Term Fiscal-Structural Plan. Guidance on whether corridor costs should be treated as cyclical or discretionary.

The legal position: Irish law contains no existing power to vary excise rates automatically. Section 96 of the Finance Act 1999 sets rates in Schedule 2; Section 104 covers administration only; Section 100(1)(k) is a narrow remission power. A Dáil Financial Resolution under the Provisional Collection of Taxes Act 1927 can activate a corridor immediately (as it did for the 2022 cuts), but it lapses after four months and has never included a formula referencing an external index. For a permanent mechanism, the Oireachtas would need to insert a new Section 96A in a Finance Act — defining the formula, bounds, and Ministerial activation power. The Cityview Press test requires that the Act set "clear declarations of policies and aims"; the more precisely the corridor parameters are defined in statute, the stronger the constitutional position. Italy's accise mobili solves this by requiring a ministerial decree to activate even when conditions are met — a model Ireland could follow.

5 The Italian model

Can the corridor pay for itself?

In March 2026, Italy announced it would activate accise mobili — "mobile excise duties" — a mechanism that uses the extra VAT revenue generated by higher pump prices to fund excise cuts. Because VAT is a percentage of the pump price, rising oil prices automatically create a fiscal windfall. Italy's insight: redirect that windfall to stabilise prices, making the intervention Exchequer-neutral by design.

Italy's approach

When pump prices rise, the State collects more VAT. The accise mobili mechanism (Law 244/2007, refined by Decree-Law 5/2023) redirects this surplus to reduce excise duty. Crucially, it requires a ministerial decree to activate — the formula and bounds are in statute, but the Minister retains discretion. Introduced in law in 2023, first activated March 2026.

Ireland's opportunity

A new Section 96A of the Finance Act 1999 could define Ireland's corridor formula, with the Minister empowered to activate adjustments by order within legislated bounds. When Brent rises above the upper bound, excise falls — but VAT on the higher wholesale price rises.
